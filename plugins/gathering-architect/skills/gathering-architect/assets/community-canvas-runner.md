# Community Canvas Workshop Runner

A facilitator's guide for running a Community Canvas workshop end-to-end. Built on the Community Canvas by Fabian Pfortmüller, Nico Luchsinger, and Sascha Mombartz (community-canvas.org, CC BY-NC-SA 4.0).

> This is a **workshop runner**, not a reproduction of the Canvas poster. The canonical poster lives at community-canvas.org and is the best printable artifact for the wall. This runner is the document the facilitator works from.

---

## How to use this runner

**Time:** 90–180 minutes for the workshop itself; 30–45 minutes of prep beforehand. The 17 themes won't all get equal time — that's by design.
**Who:** 4–8 core stakeholders. More than 8 and the conversation thins; fewer than 4 and you miss the cross-perspective that makes the Canvas worth running.
**When to use the Canvas vs. the MVC worksheet:**
- Use **MVC** (`mvc-worksheet.md`) when you're starting from zero, time-boxed, or testing whether a community is even viable.
- Use the **full Canvas** when you have a community already running (or close to it) and need to map gaps, get stakeholder alignment on a richer picture, or hand a coherent design to a community manager.
- Many teams run MVC first, then the full Canvas 4–8 weeks later once early signal is in.

**Output:** A filled Community Canvas (poster or digital) with one or two sentences per theme, plus a one-page synthesis the team can reference and revise.

## Pre-workshop prep

**One week out:**
- [ ] Decide whether you're using the printed poster (recommended for in-person) or a digital version (Miro, FigJam, etc.)
- [ ] Send the invite with purpose: "We're spending 2 hours mapping our community's design across 17 themes. Come willing to disagree productively."
- [ ] Ask attendees to skim community-canvas.org before the session (15 minutes)
- [ ] If MVC has been done, share the MVC output with attendees

**One day out:**
- [ ] Print the Canvas (if in-person) or set up the digital board with the 3 sections and 17 themes pre-laid-out
- [ ] Print or prep sticky notes (4 colors: one for each section, one for parking lot)
- [ ] Prepare a short "what is the Canvas" explainer slide for the opening
- [ ] Decide who facilitates and who captures — different jobs

**One hour out:**
- [ ] Set up room so people can see the Canvas from any seat (semicircle, U-shape, or shared screen)
- [ ] Test markers and sticky-note adhesion
- [ ] Water, coffee, snacks at the start, not the middle

## The Workshop

The Canvas is structured in three sections: **Identity** (who we are), **Experience** (what it's like to be a member), and **Structure** (how we sustain ourselves). Work in order. Don't jump to Structure before Identity is anchored — you'll design infrastructure for a community you haven't yet defined.

### Opening (10 min)

1. Welcome and name why we're here. Read the purpose statement aloud.
2. Set the rule: "We're working in two-sentence answers, not paragraphs. Speed reveals where alignment is real and where it's thin."
3. Show the Canvas. Explain the three sections briefly.
4. Establish the parking lot — anywhere a theme spawns a discussion that's bigger than this session, it gets a sticky and we move on.

### Phase 1 — Identity (30 min, 5 themes)

This phase answers "Who are we, and why do we exist?" If Identity feels rough at the end of the phase, that's the most important signal in the whole workshop. Don't paper over it.

#### Theme 1: Purpose & Goal — Why do we exist?

**Prompt to use:** "If this community disappeared tomorrow, what would members miss?"

**Common stuck point:** Confusing purpose with activities ("we run events") or audience ("we're for designers"). Push for the underlying void it fills.

### Phase 1 checkpoint (5 min)

Read the five Identity answers aloud as a group. Ask: "Does this feel like the community we want to be?" If anyone hesitates, name where. Don't fix it — just mark it. The hesitations are signal for Phase 3.

Skim Experience answers as a group. Ask: "Where does what we just described differ from what we actually have today?" Mark gaps with a different color sticky. Don't fix them now.

## Synthesis (15 min)

Step back. The Canvas is now filled. Don't congratulate yourselves yet.

1. **Walk the wall** (or scroll the board). Each person spends 3 minutes silently re-reading every theme. Looking for: contradictions, themes that ring hollow, themes where you faked alignment.

2. **Surface the three weakest themes.** Each person names them out loud. Capture in one place.

3. **Pick one theme to develop next.** Not all three — one. The three weak themes are the next quarter's design agenda; the one you pick is the next week's.

4. **Name the contradiction(s).** Most filled Canvases have at least one — a value that doesn't show up in rituals, an audience claim that doesn't match member journey, a measurement that doesn't connect to purpose. Name them so they don't ambush you later.

5. **Decide who owns the synthesis.** One person captures the filled Canvas into a one-pager within 48 hours and circulates.

---

## Post-workshop

**Within 48 hours:**
- [ ] One-page synthesis circulated to attendees
- [ ] Parking-lot items routed to owners
- [ ] Next-week action on the one theme picked

**Within 2 weeks:**
- [ ] Test one weak theme with real members (interview, survey, or observation)

**Within 8 weeks:**
- [ ] Re-run the workshop on the three weak themes only

## A worked example

A community manager at a mid-sized fintech is six months into running an internal community for ~120 engineers interested in machine learning. The MVC was done in week 2; the full Canvas is being run in week 28 because engagement has plateaued and the founder wants to know why.

**Workshop attendees:** Community manager, engineering sponsor, two active members (one from week-2 cohort, one from month-4 cohort), one self-described lurker.

**Workshop output highlights:**
- **Identity:** Purpose and Values felt strong. Member Identity was where the group stalled — the description "ML-curious engineers" was technically correct but didn't explain why an established ML practitioner would show up. Marked weak.
- **Experience:** Shared Experiences mapped cleanly to the bi-weekly demo session. Rituals were thin — no real opening or closing for the demos. Marked weak.
- **Structure:** Organization and Governance felt clear. Resources & Financing surfaced a quiet dependency on one engineering manager's time that nobody had named.

**Synthesis result:** Three weak themes (Member Identity, Rituals, Resource dependency). The one to develop next week: Member Identity, because it's upstream of recruitment and shapes everything else. Next-step action: 6 member interviews in the next 14 days, structured around "why did you stay" and "why did you almost leave."
