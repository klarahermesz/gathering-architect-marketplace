# IDOARRT Meeting Design Template

A six-field clarity tool for designing any meeting where people need to be on the same page before content starts. From Hyper Island's meeting toolkit.

I = **Intention** · D = **Desired Outcomes** · O = ~~Outcomes~~ (folded into D) · A = **Agenda** · R = **Roles** · R = **Rules** · T = **Time**

> Note on the acronym: the original is sometimes spelled IDOARRT or IDOART. We use IDOARRT (two Rs) because Rules earn a field of their own — they do the most work for the field with the least content.

---

## How to use this template

**Time:** 10–20 minutes to fill in, alone or with a co-facilitator. Then display it at the start of the meeting (project the doc, share the page, or read it aloud).
**When:** Any meeting longer than 30 minutes where the goal isn't completely obvious. Especially useful when the group is new to each other, when stakes are high, or when previous versions of this meeting have drifted.
**Output:** A one-page meeting frame everyone in the room can see and agree to before content starts.

### Why the up-front cost is worth it

Most meetings fail in the first five minutes, not the last fifty. People walk in with different assumptions about why they're there. IDOARRT surfaces those assumptions before they cause damage. The rule of thumb: if filling this in feels like overkill, the meeting probably doesn't need to happen.

### Before you start

**Meeting title:** _______________________________________________

**Date and time:** _______________________________________________

**Facilitator:** _______________________________________________

**Attendees:** _______________________________________________

## The Six Fields

### I — Intention

Why is this meeting happening? What problem does it solve or opportunity does it open? One or two sentences. If you can't write this without listing topics, the meeting doesn't have an intention yet — it has an agenda.

### D — Desired Outcomes

What does the group need to walk away with? Be concrete. "Discuss X" is not an outcome — it's an activity. "Decision on whether to proceed with X, with one named owner for next steps" is an outcome.

Aim for 2–4 outcomes. If you have more than 4, the meeting is probably too long or covers too much.

### R — Roles

Beyond facilitator, what roles does this meeting need? Common ones:

- **Decider** — who has the final call (especially on a contested decision)
- **Note-taker** — captures key decisions and action items
- **Timekeeper** — flags drift, especially in longer sessions
- **Devil's advocate** — designated voice for the counterargument (useful when the group has strong consensus pressure)
- **Subject-matter expert** — there to answer questions, not to drive

Not every meeting needs all of these. Roles should be assigned by name, not by title.

> _Note-taker:_

> _Other roles + names:_

### R — Rules

How will we behave together? This is the field most facilitators rush. It is also the field that most determines whether the meeting actually works.

A useful frame: pick 3–5 behaviors you want and 1–2 you want to prevent. Be specific. "Be respectful" is too vague to enforce. "Phones in pockets; we'll take a break every 45 minutes" is enforceable.

Common useful rules:
- One conversation at a time
- Disagreement before consensus (don't agree just to move on)
- "Yes, and" before "yes, but"
- Confidentiality boundaries (what stays in the room, what leaves)
- Decisions taken when 80% of the room is willing to live with the call, not when everyone loves it

### T — Time

Start, end, and breaks. Be honest about what's realistic — meetings that try to run 90 minutes without a break lose the back third to fatigue.

## A worked example

What a filled-in IDOARRT might look like for a 60-minute hiring debrief:

> **Intention:** Decide whether to extend an offer to candidate X for the Lead Designer role and align on a unified post-interview message.
>
> **Desired Outcomes:**
> 1. Yes/no decision on the offer, with the decider named.
> 2. If yes — agreed-on terms (level, comp range, start-date target).
> 3. If no — agreed-on feedback the recruiter can deliver.
> 4. Owner named for the next-step communication, within 24 hours.
>
> **Agenda:**
>
> | Time | Block | Owner | Maps to outcome |
> |------|-------|-------|-----------------|
> | 0–10 | Each interviewer shares their headline read | All interviewers | 1 |
> | 10–25 | Surface and discuss disagreements | Facilitator | 1 |
> | 25–40 | Decision | Decider | 1 |
> | 40–55 | Terms (if yes) / feedback (if no) | Decider + recruiter | 2 or 3 |
> | 55–60 | Owner + timeline for next-step comms | Facilitator | 4 |
>
> **Roles:** Facilitator: Klara. Decider: Adam (hiring manager). Note-taker: Maya. Devil's advocate: rotating — Klara assigns at the start.
>
> **Rules:** Each interviewer shares their read before discussion (no anchoring on first speaker). Disagreement before consensus. We decide at 80% conviction, not unanimity. Phones away.
>
> **Time:** 60 minutes. No break. If we hit minute 50 without a decision, we stop, name the blockers, and reschedule a 30-minute follow-up.

## Alignment check (do this before content starts)

Show or read the filled-in IDOARRT at the top of the meeting and ask:

**Does anyone need to change or add anything before we start?**

> _Notes from the alignment check:_

If someone wants to add an outcome you didn't plan for, decide together whether to add it, defer it to a separate meeting, or note it for parking-lot capture at the end.
