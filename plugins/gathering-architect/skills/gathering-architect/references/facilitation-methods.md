## LIBERATING STRUCTURES METHODS

Liberating Structures are easy-to-learn microstructures that enhance relational coordination and trust and quickly foster lively participation in groups of any size. Source: https://www.liberatingstructures.com/

### 1. 1-2-4-All (Liberating Structures #1)

**Purpose:** Generate questions, ideas, and suggestions with equal opportunity for all participants. Break monopolized conversations where a few voices dominate.

**Group Size:** Unlimited (5-500+)
**Time:** 10-15 minutes per cycle
**Materials:** Chairs for face-to-face interaction, paper for recording, bell for transitions

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Silent Reflection (1 minute)** — Present a meaningful question: "What opportunities do YOU see for making progress on this challenge?" Participants reflect silently and write ideas. Facilitator "firmly facilitates quiet self-reflection."

2. **Pair Discussion (2 minutes)** — Each person pairs with a partner and shares ideas. Partners build upon insights together.

3. **Foursome Development (4 minutes)** — Two pairs combine into groups of four. Share pair conversations, note similarities/differences, develop refined ideas.

4. **Whole Group Sharing (5 minutes)** — Each foursome shares ONE standout idea. Facilitator requests no repeats. Limit to 3-4 ideas in large groups.

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Use bells to signal transitions with confidence
- Maintain strict timing; do not allow slippage
- If depth is insufficient, run a second cycle
- Ring bell firmly for clear transitions
- Keep one conversation active at a time in whole group
- Graphically record emerging themes

**Variations:** Octopus format (groups of 8), Post-it notes for tracking, Connect to Design Storyboards, Remote breakout rooms

**Source:** https://www.liberatingstructures.com/1-1-2-4-all/

### 2. Troika Consulting (Liberating Structures #8)

**Purpose:** Enable peer-to-peer coaching in real time. Deepen curiosity, listening, empathy, and risk-taking.

**Group Size:** 3 people per group (groups of any quantity), 30 minutes per complete cycle
**Time:** 30 minutes per cycle (10 minutes per person)
**Materials:** 3 chairs per group arranged knee-to-knee (no tables)

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

| Step | Time | Action |
|------|------|--------|
| **REFLECTION** | 1 min | Client mentally prepares consulting question |
| **CLIENT PRESENTS** | 1-2 min | Client shares challenge and needed help |
| **CLARIFICATION** | 1-2 min | Consultants ask clarifying questions |
| **CONSULTING** | 1-2 min | **CLIENT TURNS AROUND WITH BACK FACING CONSULTANTS.** Consultants generate ideas/advice. Client listens without responding. |
| **CLIENT FEEDBACK** | 4-5 min | Client turns back around, shares what was most valuable |
| **REPEAT FOR NEXT PERSON** | | |

**The "No Eye Contact" Rule:** When client faces away, consultants can be bolder and more creative. Client can listen without pressure to defend. This shifts to "what do you see that they do not see?"

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Mix roles, functions, backgrounds intentionally
- Encourage consultants to be "respectfully provocative"
- Maintain psychological safety; share information judiciously
- Normalize as routine part of meetings
- Keep client's turned back position sacred

**Variations:** Combine with 15% Solutions, Q-Storming (questions only), Extended rounds

**Source:** https://www.liberatingstructures.com/8-troika-consulting/

### 3. 15% Solutions

**Purpose:** Identify immediate actionable steps within current authority and resources. Reduce powerlessness.

**Group Size:** Unlimited (2-4 person small groups optimal)
**Time:** 20 minutes total
**Materials:** Chairs, optional paper/pens, open space

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Individual Reflection (5 minutes)** — Ask: "What is your 15 percent? Where do you have discretion and freedom to act?" Each person writes ideas.

2. **Small Group Sharing (3 minutes per person)** — Each individual presents ideas one at a time.

3. **Peer Consultation (5-7 minutes per person)** — Group members ask clarifying questions and offer advice. Focus: "What could you do with what you already have?"

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Verify items fall within individual discretion
- Expect small changes to trigger larger shifts
- Share stories about individual actions producing results
- Normalize reinvention and incremental improvement
- Redirect proposals outside someone's authority

**Variations:** Rapid cycles, Department integration, Challenge-specific, Team version

**Source:** https://www.liberatingstructures.com/7-15-solutions/

### 4. Wicked Questions

**Purpose:** Articulate paradoxical challenges requiring simultaneous pursuit of opposing strategies. Shift "either/or" thinking to "both/and."

**Group Size:** 4-6 people per group, 25-30 minutes
**Time:** 5 min intro + 5 min generation + 5 min selection + 10 min refinement
**Materials:** Flipchart paper, markers, tables (optional)

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Introduce Paradox (5 minutes)** — Explain concept with examples. Provide template: "How is it that we are _____ and we are _____ simultaneously?"

2. **Generate Questions (5 minutes)** — Groups create opposing-yet-complementary strategy pairs. Record all questions.

3. **Select & Share (5 minutes)** — Groups share 1-2 most impactful questions to whole group.

4. **Refine (10 minutes)** — Whole group examines all questions. Refine wording to be appreciative on both sides. Identify strongest/most generative.

**Example Wicked Questions:**
- "How do you raise children to be deeply family-connected yet fiercely independent simultaneously?"
- "How do we maintain consistency in values while adapting practices to different regions?"
- "How are we simultaneously one unified organization and uniquely adapted to each local context?"

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Frame both sides appreciatively (not as blame)
- Use diverse examples for accessibility
- Work in quick cycles, iterating toward stronger questions
- Draw on field experience
- Avoid data-answerable questions

**Variations:** Pre-crafted questions, Stacked with other methods, Iterative rounds

**Source:** https://www.liberatingstructures.com/4-wicked-questions/

### 5. TRIZ (Making Space with Innovation)

**Purpose:** Identify counterproductive activities that must be stopped to make progress. Challenge sacred cows safely.

**Group Size:** 4-7 per group, 35 minutes
**Time:** 5 min intro + 10 min + 10 min + 10 min
**Materials:** Flipchart paper, markers, tables, chairs

**Three-Step Facilitation Process:**

1. **Step 1 (10 minutes):** Ask: "Make a list of all you can do to make sure you achieve the WORST result imaginable with respect to [your top strategy]." Groups brainstorm everything counterproductive.

2. **Step 2 (10 minutes):** Ask: "Is there anything we are currently doing that resembles this item?" Groups create list of actual counterproductive activities. This is the hard truth moment.

3. **Step 3 (10 minutes):** Ask: "What first steps will help you STOP what you know creates undesirable results?" Groups identify concrete actions to eliminate harmful activities.

**Core Question:** "What must we STOP doing to make progress on our deepest purpose?"

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Maintain "seriously fun" spirit throughout
- Reject suggestions about starting new initiatives—focus strictly on stopping
- Monitor groups showing confusion; re-center on purpose
- Help identify harmful parallels to current practices
- Document using "I will stop" and "we will stop" language
- Celebrate courage to identify counterproductive activities

**Variations:** Organizational level, Team level, Sequence after Wicked Questions

**Source:** https://www.liberatingstructures.com/6-making-space-with-triz/

### 6. Impromptu Networking

**Purpose:** Stimulate rapid sharing of challenges and mutual connection-building. Works with groups of any size.

**Group Size:** Unlimited (5-500+), all in pairs
**Time:** 20 minutes total (3 rounds of ~5 minutes each)
**Materials:** Open floor space, bell for transitions, optional: cards with questions

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Introduction (2 minutes)** — Explain structure: pairs will have 2 minutes each to answer questions, then rotate. Three rounds total (6 minutes of talking per person).

2. **Round 1 (2 min per person + transition)** — Pair randomly. Pose Question 1: "What big challenge do you bring to this gathering?" Each speaks 2 minutes without interruption.

3. **Round 1 Rotation (1 minute)** — Ring bell. Pairs dissolve; regroup with new partner.

4. **Round 2 (2 min per person + transition)** — Same structure. Question 2: "What do you hope to get from and give this group?"

5. **Round 2 Rotation (1 minute)** — Form new pairs.

6. **Round 3 (2 min per person)** — Question 3 (context-specific): "What hunch are you testing?" / "What problem are you solving?"

7. **Final Bell (1 minute)** — Conclude.

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Design open but focused questions
- Keep time strictly; ring bell firmly
- Require three complete rounds (two is insufficient)
- Include serious play in tone
- Stand where you can see everyone
- Circulate visibly enjoying the process

**Variations:** Liquid Courage (sentence completions), Outdoor settings, Large group protocol, Follow-up with 15% Solutions

**Source:** https://www.liberatingstructures.com/2-impromptu-networking/

### 7. Min Specs (Minimum Specifications)

**Purpose:** Identify only essential rules needed for success. Eliminate unnecessary constraints. Add both freedom AND responsibility.

**Group Size:** 4-7 per group, 35-50 minutes
**Time:** 1 min individual + 5 min consolidation + 15 min reduction round 1 + 15 min reduction round 2
**Materials:** Flipchart paper, markers, tables, chairs

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Individual Max Specs (1 minute)** — Ask: "What are ALL the do's and don'ts that must be respected to achieve [our purpose]?" Each person writes every rule they think necessary.

2. **Group Consolidation (5 minutes)** — Groups combine lists, expand to most comprehensive Max Specs list. One person records on flipchart.

3. **First Reduction Round (15 minutes)** — Test each specification against core purpose. For each: "If we violated this rule, could we still achieve our purpose?" If YES, eliminate it. Keep remaining.

4. **Second Reduction Round (15 minutes)** — Repeat on remaining specifications. Continue eliminating until reaching 2-5 core specifications.

5. **Cross-Group Consolidation (optional 15 minutes)** — Compare Min Specs across groups. Identify themes. Develop unified final list.

**Key Elimination Question:** "If we broke or ignored this rule, could we still achieve our purpose?"

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Ground discussion in tangible challenges, not abstract concepts
- Ruthlessly eliminate rules; resist "max spec creep"
- Prioritize field experience over theoretical arguments
- Expect 3-5 final Min Specs for most challenges
- Celebrate insight: fewer rules often enable better performance

**Variations:** Organizational change, Project launch, Team formation, Cross-functional

**Source:** https://www.liberatingstructures.com/14-min-specs/

### 8. Celebrity Interview

**Purpose:** Build deeper understanding with formal leaders. Transform passive presentations into engaging narratives. Connect large audiences with leaders via talk-show format.

**Group Size:** Unlimited audience, 2 primary participants (interviewer + celebrity/leader)
**Time:** 35-60 minutes total (3 min opening + 15-30 min interview + 5-10 min question generation + 5-10 min follow-up + 1-2 min closing)
**Materials:** Theater/classroom seating, 2 comfortable chairs/bar stools at front, lapel microphones, 3x5 cards for questions, pens

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

| Phase | Time | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| **OPENING** | 3 min | Introduce celebrity and topic warmly. Set tone for authentic conversation. |
| **INTERVIEW** | 15-30 min | Interviewer poses substantive questions seeking stories/details. Adopt engaging talk-show style (warm, curious, probing). Celebrity responds authentically. |
| **QUESTION GENERATION** | 5-10 min | Audience writes follow-up questions on 3x5 cards. Facilitate voting on which questions matter most. |
| **FOLLOW-UP** | 5-10 min | Review question patterns. Ask additional substantive questions. Continue conversation style. |
| **CLOSING** | 1-2 min | Thank celebrity. Closing remarks. |

**Recommended Opening Questions:**
- "What first inspired you in this work?"
- "What challenges you in this work?"
- "What keeps you going in this work?"
- "What do you hope can happen for us in this work?"

**Interviewer Stance:**
- Seek stories and specific examples (not abstract philosophy)
- Ask follow-up: "Can you give an example?"
- Request personal relevance: "Why is this important to YOU?"
- Adopt talk-show energy: lean forward, show genuine curiosity
- Avoid lengthy introductions
- Repeatedly request stories and specific examples

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Avoid lengthy introductions beforehand
- Provide questions to celebrity in advance
- Select engaging, skilled interviewer
- Create café-like ambiance
- Manage audience energy with humor and engagement

**Variations:** Virtual format, Panel format, Fishbowl style, Preceded by other methods

**Source:** https://www.liberatingstructures.com/22-celebrity-interview/

## WORLD CAFÉ

**Purpose:** Host meaningful conversations moving beyond small talk. Harvest collective intelligence. Enable cross-pollination of ideas.

**Group Size:** 10-40 optimal, 4-6 per table
**Time:** 60-120 minutes (15-20 min setup + 60 min conversations + 15-30 min harvest)
**Materials:** Round tables covered with paper, colored markers, refreshments, flowers, optional talking stick, flipchart for harvest, comfortable chairs

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Setup (20 minutes before)** — Arrange tables scattered (not rows). Cover with white paper. Place markers, flowers, refreshments at each table. Set welcoming café atmosphere.

2. **Opening & Welcome (5-10 minutes)** — Welcome group. Introduce topic. Explain World Café process. Explain table host role. Invite informal seating at tables (4-6 per table).

3. **Round 1 (20 minutes)** — Groups discuss first question (same for all or varied). Table host supports flow without leading. One person records in words and drawings on table paper. Participants listen to understand.

4. **Round 1 Transition (2 minutes)** — Ring bell. One person stays as table host. All others stand and move to new table.

5. **Round 2 (20 minutes)** — Repeat at new tables. Table host brings incoming group up to speed. Same or deepening question.

6. **Round 2 Transition (2 minutes)** — Ring bell. Form new pairs again.

7. **Round 3 (20 minutes)** — Final round. Question can be synthesis or action question. Many will know multiple people.

8. **Harvesting (15-30 minutes)** — Reconvene whole group. Invite participants to share key insights, themes, patterns, questions that emerged across tables. Facilitator or recorder synthesizes on flipchart. Each table shares few key insights.

**Question Design for Rounds:**
- **Round 1:** Open, generative, personal connection ("What drew you to this conversation?")
- **Round 2:** Deepening ("What new insights are you having?")
- **Round 3:** Action or synthesis ("What possibilities are we creating together?")

**Table Host Role:**
- Welcome incoming participants warmly
- Briefly summarize previous round (1-2 minutes)
- Do NOT direct or lead the new conversation
- Support flow by asking follow-up if silence emerges
- Encourage all voices
- Note key themes on the paper

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Design compelling, open-ended questions
- Use café ambiance to create informality and comfort
- Ring transitions clearly but gently
- Position as orchestrator, not expert
- In harvesting, look for patterns and tensions
- Invite participants to look at table paper drawings and notes
- Create psychological safety in opening

**Variations:** Fishbowl World Café, Standing Café, Progressive question sequence, Stakeholder worlds, Virtual World Café

**Source:** https://www.sessionlab.com/methods/world-cafe

## OPEN SPACE TECHNOLOGY

Developed by Harrison Owen. A self-organizing methodology for addressing complex issues where outcomes cannot be predetermined and diverse stakeholders must work together.

**Purpose:** Address complex multifaceted issues. Include all stakeholders meaningfully. Build commitment through self-organization. Navigate conflict and diversity toward solutions.

**Group Size:** 5-1,500+ (5 minimum but less magic; 50-150 optimal)
**Time:** Half-day (4 hours) to multiple days (longer = more transformative)
**Materials:** Chairs for concentric circles, Agenda Wall, News Wall, newsprint and markers, masking tape, computers/printers (optional), timer, comfortable breakout spaces

**The Four Principles:**

1. **Whoever comes is the right people** — Those present have the care and competence needed. No worry about missing stakeholders.

2. **Whatever happens is the only thing that could have happened** — Accept outcomes as inevitable. Enables presence and acceptance.

3. **Whenever it starts is the right time** — Honors organic timing of creativity. Rejects rigid scheduling.

4. **When it's over, it's over** — Honors natural completion. Prevents artificial extension.

**The Law of Two Feet:** "You, and only you, know when you are learning and contributing as much as you can. If you find yourself neither learning nor contributing, use your two feet to go somewhere you can learn and contribute more." Participants move between sessions based on their learning and contribution.

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Pre-Event (1-2 weeks before)** — Craft focusing statement (positive framing). Invite all stakeholders with theme, date, place, time. Send minimal background reading. Request participants come with one issue they care about.

2. **Event Setup (1-2 hours before)** — Arrange chairs in concentric circles. Create Agenda Wall ("AGENDA: AM, PM"). Create News Wall ("NEWS" or "OUTCOMES"). Place newsprint/markers in center circle. Place masking tape near Agenda Wall.

3. **Opening Circle (30-60 minutes)**:
   - Welcome (5 min) — Warm greeting
   - Present Theme (5 min) — Explain focusing question
   - Explain Process (5 min) — All will create agenda
   - Teach Principles (10 min) — Explain all four + Law of Two Feet
   - Silent Reflection (5-10 min) — Participants reflect on their issue
   - Invite Marketplace (ongoing) — Anyone steps into center, writes topic, announces it, chooses time/location, states name, tapes to wall

4. **The Marketplace (Topic Creation Phase)** — Participant steps into center: writes topic/question on newsprint, announces briefly (30 seconds max), chooses time slot, chooses location, states name (they're session convener), tapes to wall. Process continues until all topics posted. Participants sign up using Law of Two Feet.

5. **Session Process (runs throughout)** — Breakout sessions (60-90 min typically) at chosen locations. Session convener facilitates (or participants self-organize). Discussion. Volunteer records notes on newsprint. Focus: Listen, Think, Solve.

6. **Note Posting (after each session)** — Convener posts session notes on News Wall or central collection. Notes become part of proceedings.

7. **Closing Circle (1-2 hours before end)** — Reconvene in concentric circles. Dialogue round: "What was significant for you?" No discussion/debate; just witnessing. Allow passes. Capture themes on flipchart. Discuss immediate actions/commitments. Brief closing remarks.

8. **Proceedings (immediately after)** — Compile all session notes and outcomes. Include participant contact information. Distribute within 48 hours.

**Facilitator (Convener) Tips:**

**During Marketplace:**
- Stand in center with confidence and energy
- Encourage topics, especially from quieter voices
- Hold space for awkward silence—let it birth topics
- Interrupt over-explanation: "Great! Post it!"
- Help reluctant voices: "What's the question you're bringing?"

**During Sessions:**
- Circulate; observe; note themes
- Use minimal intervention
- Trust the Law of Two Feet
- Do not assign session facilitators
- Ensure newsprint posted on News Wall after each session

**During Closing Circle:**
- Set up circle clearly
- Emphasize: "This is not debate—just witnessing"
- Capture key themes on visible flipchart
- Ask: "What's one commitment you're making?"

**Variations:** One-day, Smaller groups, Online/virtual, Pre-work with provocative questions, Multi-site, Follow-up calls

**Source:** https://openspaceworld.org/wp2/what-is/ and https://www.openspaceworld.org/files/tmnfiles/2pageos.htm

## FISHBOWL DISCUSSION

**Purpose:** Develop active listening and observation skills. Engage meaningful conversations while observers learn. Deepen understanding through role-switching.

**Group Size:** 10-20 optimal, Inner circle 4-6, Outer circle remainder
**Time:** 45-60 minutes (15 min round 1 + 5 min swap + 15 min round 2 + 10-15 min debrief)
**Materials:** Chairs in two concentric circles, Observer handouts (optional), Pens, Flipchart (optional), 3-5 prepared discussion questions

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Preparation** — Design 3-5 guiding questions. Identify diverse inner circle participants. Prepare observer handouts (optional). Brief inner circle on roles.

2. **Setup (10 minutes)** — Arrange chairs in two concentric circles. Inner: 4-6 chairs facing each other. Outer: remaining chairs to see and hear clearly. Display prepared questions visibly. Ask for volunteers or assign to inner circle (mix perspectives).

3. **Introduction (3-5 minutes)** — Welcome. Explain structure. Introduce topic and questions. Set norms: "Inner circle: talk to each other, not the outer circle. Use specific examples. Outer circle: listen carefully, take notes on discussion content, tone, and patterns."

4. **Round 1 Discussion (15 minutes)** — Inner circle discusses using prepared questions. Facilitator may ask clarifying questions if discussion stalls. Outer circle observes silently and takes notes. Facilitator enforces "no speeches" rule—keep contributions conversational.

5. **Role Swap (5 minutes)** — Ring bell. Inner circle moves to outer. Outer moves to inner. Brief debrief: "Inner circle, think about what you were experiencing. Outer circle, you're entering now."

6. **Round 2 Discussion (15 minutes)** — New inner circle discusses same or new deepening question. Repeat conversation structure. New outer circle observes.

7. **Whole Group Debrief (10-15 minutes)** — Reconvene in whole group or semi-circle. Ask reflection questions: "What patterns did you notice?" "What surprised you?" "What was it like to observe vs. participate?" Invite volunteer sharing.

**Discussion Facilitation Moves (for inner circle):**
- "Can you give a specific example?"
- "Tell us more about that."
- "How does that connect to what [other person] just said?"
- "What evidence supports that?"
- "What would need to be true for that to work?"
- **STOP over-explanation:** "That's helpful; let's hear from someone else"

**Debrief Questions:**
- "What did you notice about the conversation?"
- "What themes or patterns emerged?"
- "What was challenging about listening without talking?"
- "How did it feel to be in the inner circle?"
- "What did observing teach you about this topic?"

**Variations:** Open Fishbowl (one empty chair that outer circle can enter), Rotating Fishbowl, Fishbowl with chat, Multi-round, Goldfish Bowl (standing, tighter circle)

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Set norms clearly
- Enforce focus; no speeches
- Manage dominators gently
- Ask for evidence
- Surface disagreement
- Note patterns for debrief
- Create psychological safety

**Source:** https://www.sessionlab.com/methods/fishbowl

## MASTERMIND GROUPS

Small group of like-minded people committed to supporting each other's growth, problem-solving, and success. Members serve as both advisors and accountable partners.

**Purpose:** Provide peer coaching and collaborative problem-solving. Build accountability on goals. Access diverse expertise. Create container for vulnerability.

**Group Size:** 4-6 ideal, 3 minimum, 8-10 maximum
**Duration:** Long-term relationships (3-12 months minimum)
**Frequency:** Monthly or bi-weekly standing commitments
**Time Per Session:** 90 minutes typical
**Per Person's "Hot Seat":** 20-30 minutes focused time
**Materials:** Round table or circle of comfortable chairs, Timer, Optional: Notebook for recording commitments, Shared document for tracking action items, Refreshments

**Typical 90-Minute Session Structure:**

| Phase | Time | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| **CHECK-IN** | 5 min | Brief opening. Everyone: "What are you bringing today?" (1 sentence each) |
| **PERSON 1 HOT SEAT** | 25 min | 5 min share challenge/goal, 15 min peer advisory, 5 min commitment and next steps |
| **BREAK** | 5 min | Move, refresh |
| **PERSON 2 HOT SEAT** | 25 min | Same as above |
| **BREAK** | 5 min | (Optional) |
| **PERSON 3 HOT SEAT** | 25 min | Same as above |
| **CHECK-OUT** | 5 min | "One commitment I'm making" or "One thing I'm taking forward" |

**Pre-Group Setup:**
1. Recruit 4-6 aligned people (similar stage, shared challenges, growth mindset)
2. Set expectations clearly: frequency, duration, confidentiality, commitment, cost (if any)
3. Schedule recurring sessions in advance
4. Create shared document for tracking commitments

**Core Facilitation: The Hot Seat Rounds**

**PERSON IN HOT SEAT (5 minutes: Sharing Challenge/Goal):**
- "What's on your mind? What do you need support with?"
- Share specific challenge, goal, or decision point
- Be vulnerable and honest
- Group listens without interruption
- Group refrains from solutions in this phase
- This person owns the problem; others are witnesses

**ADVISORS' TURN (15 minutes: Peer Advisory):**
- Group members ask clarifying questions first:
  - "What have you already tried?"
  - "What's your desired outcome?"
  - "What's the real fear underneath?"
- Then shift to:
  - Offering reflections: "What I'm hearing is..."
  - Sharing relevant experiences: "In my situation, I..."
  - Proposing ideas: "What if you tried...?"
- Advisor's Job:
  - Listen for what's not being said
  - Ask questions that deepen thinking
  - Share hard truths with compassion
  - Offer perspective from outside the situation
  - Honor the person's autonomy and choice

**HOT SEAT PERSON'S TURN (5 minutes: Commitments & Next Steps):**
- "Based on this conversation, what are you committing to?"
- Make specific, measurable commitments
- Share by when: "By our next meeting, I will..."
- Others hold them accountable

**FULL CYCLE:**
- Repeat for each person until all have had hot seat time
- Keep timing sacred (use timer visibly)

**Facilitator Tips:**

**For Advisors:**
- Ask questions, not statements
- Avoid "fixing" the person's problem
- Share experiences, not prescriptions
- Notice patterns they might not see
- Hold both compassion and candor
- Speak as a peer, not an expert

**For Hot Seat Person:**
- Be specific about what you need
- Listen to understand, not to defend
- Ask for what would help most
- Make commitments you'll keep
- Report back on progress at next meeting

**For Facilitator/Group:**
- Keep time sacred—timer visible
- Protect equal speaking time
- Manage dominators gently
- Track commitments and follow up
- Celebrate wins and progress
- Refresh group agreements periodically

**Variations:** Quarterly deep dives, Topic-based meetings, Expert guests, Cohort model, Peer mentoring, Accountability checkpoints

**Source:** https://voltagecontrol.com/events/facilitation-lab-facilitating-mastermind-conversations/

## SHOW AND TELL / DEMO SESSIONS

Get deeper understanding of stakeholders' perspectives through object-based sharing.

**Purpose:** Understand perspectives via metaphor and narrative. Build empathy across teams. Celebrate work in progress. Create safe space for emotional expression.

**Group Size:** 2-15 optimal (5-8 for intimacy)
**Time:** 15-30 minutes total (2-3 min per person + group response time)
**Materials:** Objects brought by participants, Whiteboard/flipchart and markers, Comfortable seating in circle, Quiet, safe space

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Preparation (Before session)** — Invite: "Bring an object that represents your perspective on [topic]." Keep objects hidden. Set expectation: "2-3 minutes to show and explain. All perspectives valued."

2. **Set the Stage (2-3 minutes)** — Welcome. Display topic/prompt on whiteboard. Optional: sketch visual representations. Explain: "Each person shows their object and explains why. Our job is listen with curiosity. There are no right or wrong objects."

3. **Model Vulnerability (optional 2-3 minutes)** — If appropriate, facilitator shares own object first. Significantly increases safety and participation.

4. **Invite Volunteers (2-3 minutes per person)** — Ask: "Who'd like to share first?" Each person: Takes out object, shows it, explains why they chose it, describes what it represents about their perspective. Keep to 2-3 minutes. Listen intently. Take visual notes or sketches as they speak.

5. **Synthesize Themes (5-10 minutes)** — After all shared, facilitator summarizes on whiteboard: "Here are the themes I'm noticing..." (excitement, concerns, doubt, uncertainty, possibility, etc.). Ask: "What patterns do you see?"

6. **Deepen Understanding (5-10 minutes)** — Ask follow-up questions: "What surprised you?" "What connections do you see?" "What shifted for you?" Invite people to notice what objects were chosen and by whom.

**Active Listening & Documentation:**
- Listen for emotional language, hesitations, values, concerns, hopes
- Note similarities, differences, surprising elements
- Sketch simple visual representations alongside notes
- Summarize back: "It sounds like... Is that right?"

**Variations:** Structured feedback ("I like, I wish, What if?"), Gallery walk, Metaphor extended, Artifact swap, Digital version with photos and explanations

**Feedback Format: "I Like, I Wish, What If?"**
- **I Like:** "I like that you chose something representing hope..."
- **I Wish:** "I wish everyone felt that level of confidence..."
- **What If:** "What if we used this as a guiding image for our project?"

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Model vulnerability; share your object first if possible
- Maintain silence after someone shares for reflection
- Ask follow-up questions
- Validate all contributions
- Synthesize across contributions visibly
- Honor emotional content
- Document visually

**Source:** https://www.sessionlab.com/methods/show-and-tell

## MEANINGFUL CHECK-INS AND CHECK-OUTS

Rituals that open and close sessions with purpose. Create presence, build connection, enable reflection without forcing lengthy processing.

**Purpose:** Check-ins establish presence, focus, group commitment at opening. Check-outs enable symbolic closure and personal reflection at closing. Build connection and psychological safety.

**Group Size:** 2-40 participants
**Time:** 5-30 minutes for check-in, 10-20 minutes for check-out (varies by group size and question depth)
**Materials:** Chairs in circle, Optional: talking stick, Optional: flipchart, Optional: music

**Alternative Check-In Questions:**

**Scaled Questions:**
- "On a scale of 1-10, how present are you today?"
- "Rate your energy from low to high"
- "How aligned are you with the purpose of this meeting?"

**Weather/Temperature Questions:**
- "If today were a weather forecast for your mood, what would it be?"
- "What temperature are you today and why?"
- "If you were a season, which would you be right now?"

**Object-Based Questions:**
- "Describe your personality using three unrelated objects"
- "What object from this room represents how you're feeling?"
- "Choose one word that describes your energy today"

**Personal Connection Questions:**
- "What are you grateful for right now?"
- "What's one thing that made you smile recently?"
- "Who inspires you and why?"

**Work-Related Check-Ins:**
- "What's your intention for this session?"
- "What question are you holding?"
- "What brought you here today?"
- "What do you hope to take away from this time together?"

**Playful/Energizing Check-Ins:**
- "One word that describes your mood?"
- "Emoji that captures your vibe?"
- "Animal spirit that matches your energy?"
- "Song that's stuck in your head?"

**Check-Out Question Options:**

**Reflection-Based:**
- "One insight you're taking with you?"
- "One commitment you're making?"
- "What will you do differently based on this session?"
- "What's one thing you'll remember?"

**Gratitude-Based:**
- "Who or what are you grateful for from this session?"
- "What will you appreciate about today?"
- "One compliment for the group?"

**Integration-Based:**
- "How will you use what you learned?"
- "What's next for you?"
- "One action you'll take this week?"

**Poetic:**
- "Complete this: 'I came seeking ___; I'm leaving with ___'"
- "If this session were a plant, it would be..."
- "One word that will remind you of today?"

**Step-by-Step Facilitation for Check-In:**

1. **Framing (1-2 minutes)** — Invite circle. Explain: "We're opening by checking in. Chance to be present and connect. Here's the question..." Clarify: "Share whatever feels true. You can pass. Aim for 1-2 sentences."

2. **Optional Object Pass (0 minutes)** — If using, place meaningful object in center. Explain: "When you're speaking, hold this. It's our talking piece."

3. **Going Around Circle (varies)** — Each person shares in order (or randomly). Keep to 1-2 sentences. Listen with full attention. Allow passes. Keep momentum.

4. **Transition to Work (1 minute)** — Brief summary: "I hear excitement, some uncertainty, commitment. Let's carry that into our time together." Move into substantive work.

**Step-by-Step Facilitation for Check-Out:**

1. **Transition (1-2 minutes)** — Pause work 10-15 minutes before end. Invite circle. Explain: "Chance to reflect on what we're taking with us."

2. **Offer Question (0 minutes)** — Pose check-out question. Example: "One word that captures what you're leaving with?"

3. **Going Around Circle (varies)** — Each person shares (1-2 sentences max). Full attention. Listen for wisdom and closure. Allow passes. Celebrate insights briefly.

4. **Closing Acknowledgment (1-2 minutes)** — Optional: "Thank you for being present. The work we did here matters. Go forward with..."

**Facilitator Tips:**

**General:**
- Model brevity: go first with example
- "You can pass at any time"
- Full attention; put away phones
- Allow silence; don't rush gaps
- Validate all contributions
- Notice patterns (what themes emerge?)

**For Check-Ins:**
- Keep energy light
- Choose questions aligned with session purpose
- Use playful questions for virtual meetings
- Save deepest questions for mid-session

**For Check-Outs:**
- Allow more reflection time at end
- Offer choice: "One word or one sentence"
- Capture on flipchart if appropriate
- Honor silence; people need time
- Celebrate wins and learning

**Variations (for large groups 20+):**
- Pair/share: People turn to neighbor, then one shares to larger group
- Small group circles: Break into 3-4 circles; simultaneous check-in/out
- Silent reflection: People write on card; facilitator reads key ones
- Popcorn style: Anyone can share when ready (not forced order)

**For Virtual/Hybrid:**
- Chat check-in: People type; facilitator reads aloud
- Breakout rooms: Small groups simultaneously
- Emoji reactions: Use reaction buttons for mood
- Shared doc: Type responses while facilitator reads some aloud
- Hybrid option: In-person speak first, then each remote person
- Visual check-in: People hold up fingers (1-10 energy); no words

**Source:** https://hyperisland.com/en/toolbox/check-in-check-out

### 1. IDOARRT Meeting Design

Simple framework for establishing clarity at meeting start. Answers fundamental questions participants have.

**Purpose:** Establish transparency and shared understanding. Reduce confusion and hidden agendas. Create psychological safety through clarity.

**Group Size:** Any size (1-500+)
**Time:** 10-15 minutes for introduction and agreement
**Materials:** Flipchart or slide visible to all, Markers

**The Six Components:**

| Component | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **INTENTION** | Why are we meeting? What problem are we solving? | "We're here to redesign our onboarding process to reduce time-to-productivity and improve new hire experience" |
| **DESIRED OUTCOME(S)** | What specific results will we achieve by end of meeting? | "By 2pm, we will have identified 3 new onboarding workflows and assigned owners to each" |
| **AGENDA** | What activities will move us toward the outcome? In what order? | 1. Warmup (10 min), 2. Current state mapping (20 min), 3. Ideation (30 min), 4. Prototyping (20 min), 5. Commitments (10 min) |
| **RULES** | What are our norms and boundaries? | "Phones away, full participation, ideas before criticism, all voices heard, confidentiality" |
| **ROLES & RESPONSIBILITIES** | Who is facilitating? Documenting? Timekeeping? | "Sarah facilitates. Marcus keeps time. Jen documents." |
| **TIME** | How long is this meeting? When do we break? | "2 hours total. We break at 12:45. We end at 2:00pm sharp." |

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Introduce Framework (2 minutes)** — "I want to make sure we're all on the same page. We're going to walk through six things that will help us work together effectively today." Point to the six components.

2. **Intention (2 minutes)** — "Why are we here? What problem or opportunity brings us together?" Ask group; listen; clarify and refine until agreement.

3. **Desired Outcome (2 minutes)** — "What specific results will we have by the end? Success looks like..." Invite input; refine until clear.

4. **Agenda (2 minutes)** — "How will we spend our time to reach that outcome?" Lay out sequence. Ask: "Does this make sense?" Adjust if needed.

5. **Rules (2 minutes)** — "What ground rules will help us do our best work?" Suggest a few ("Phones away, full presence, respect all opinions, one person speaks at a time"). Invite: "What else?" Refine together.

6. **Roles & Responsibilities (2 minutes)** — "Who's doing what?" Clarify facilitator. Get volunteers for timekeeping and documentation.

7. **Time (1 minute)** — "We're meeting from [start time] to [end time]. We'll take a break at [time]. We'll wrap up at [time]." Write times clearly.

8. **Group Agreement (1 minute)** — "Does this all make sense? Are we aligned?" Check for nods/agreement. "Great, let's get started."

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Keep it quick
- Invite input; co-create with group
- Be flexible; incorporate suggestions
- Reference it during meeting if needed
- Adjust mid-meeting if needed
- Keep IDOARRT visible throughout for reference

**Variations:** Simplified version (Intention and Desired Outcome only), Detailed version, Participant-created, Visual display

**Source:** https://www.sessionlab.com/methods/idoarrt-meeting-design

### 2. Feedback: Start, Stop, Continue

Helps teams build trust and openness by providing structured peer feedback on behaviors and contributions.

**Purpose:** Build trust and psychological safety in established teams. Give constructive feedback in structured format. Surface implicit team dynamics. Enable growth-oriented conversation.

**Group Size:** 4-6 optimal, up to 8-10 (intimacy decreases), **Only for mature groups** (3+ months working together)
**Time:** 60-120 minutes total (10 min framing + 30-50 min delivery + 15-30 min processing)
**Materials:** Post-it notes (one color), Pens/markers, Safe, private space

**Maturity Requirement:** Only use with established groups. Newer teams should start with "Current Strongest Impression" or "I Appreciate" feedback methods first.

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Frame the Exercise (3-5 minutes)** — Explain: "We're going to give each other feedback using the Start, Stop, Continue framework. This is about behaviors and contributions, not character judgments. We're doing this to support each other's growth."

2. **Write Feedback (10 minutes)** — Each person writes on post-its for every other team member: "To [name]: START doing... STOP doing... CONTINUE doing..." Silent, individual writing. You don't need to address all three if you don't have relevant feedback.

3. **Deliver Feedback (40-60 minutes)** — Go person by person. All others share their post-its (read aloud, then hand to recipient). Person receiving listens and takes notes. Receiver does NOT respond, defend, or explain. They just listen. After all feedback delivered, brief reflection: "What are you hearing?" Rotate until everyone has received feedback.

4. **Group Reflection (15-30 minutes)** — After all feedback delivered: "What patterns did you notice?" "What surprised you?" "What will you do with this feedback?" Invite voluntary sharing and learning.

**Delivery Protocol:**
- Person reads feedback aloud
- Hands post-it to recipient
- Recipient listens silently
- No in-the-moment discussion
- Move through all team members before debrief

**Feedback Template:**
- **START:** "I'd like you to start [specific behavior]..."
- **STOP:** "I'd like you to stop [specific behavior]..."
- **CONTINUE:** "Keep doing [specific behavior]..."

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Front-load safety
- Model vulnerability if facilitator participates
- Enforce listening: "No explaining; just listen and take notes"
- Look for patterns
- Follow up individually after exercise
- Celebrate growth

**Variations:** Anonymous version, Appreciative version (Start and Continue only), Topic-specific, Recurring monthly cycles, Hybrid delivery with 1-1 conversations

**Source:** https://www.sessionlab.com/methods/feedback-start-stop-continue

### 3. Team Self-Assessment

Structured workshop where teams reflect on their functioning across six dimensions and make commitments for improvement.

**Purpose:** Surface implicit team dynamics and make explicit. Enable honest conversations about how they work together. Identify strengths to celebrate and areas for development. Create action plans for improvement.

**Group Size:** 2-10 people (same team), 60-120 minutes
**The Six Dimensions:**
1. Team Relations & Environment
2. Information & Decision-Making
3. Responsibility & Accountability
4. Learning & Individual Purpose
5. Collective Purpose
6. Profit & Productivity

**Materials:** Flipchart paper (one per team member, pre-divided into 6 sections), Post-it notes (same color), Black markers, Colored sticky dots, Masking tape, Optional: Digital version (Google Docs, Mural)

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Introduce Framework (3-5 minutes)** — Explain purpose: "We're going to reflect on how we work together as a team. We'll look at six dimensions that are important to team health." Show dimensions. Emphasize: "We're analyzing ourselves as a system, not blaming individuals."

2. **Individual Reflection (30 minutes)** — Each person works silently. Receives flipchart divided into 6 sections (one per dimension). For each, write post-it statements completing: "As a team, we..." Statements can be positive (right side), developmental (left side), or mixed. Encourage 3-5 statements per dimension.

3. **Group Sharing (20 minutes)** — Go dimension by dimension. Each person reads 2-3 statements (or key ones). All statements posted on master chart. Facilitator records without judgment. No explanation needed; just sharing what they wrote.

4. **Clustering (15 minutes)** — Invite volunteer to group similar statements silently. Once done, ask: "Does this grouping make sense?" Allow adjustments. Goal: Reduce duplications and group themes.

5. **Dot Voting (10 minutes)** — Give each person 3-5 colored dots. Invite them to vote on statements they want to prioritize for change/celebration/action: "Place your dots on statements most important to address." Multiple votes on same statement allowed. Goal: Identify top 3-5 statements for action.

6. **Action Planning (15-20 minutes)** — Review top-voted statements. For each, ask: "What will we do about this?" Create action items: "By [date], we will..." Assign owners. Photograph results. Transfer to shared document.

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Frame as system analysis, not blaming
- Protect silence during individual reflection
- Stay neutral; don't judge statements
- Celebrate strengths; don't only focus on problems
- Create follow-up: "We'll revisit in 30 days"
- Digital option for remote/hybrid teams

**Variations:** Ongoing assessment (quarterly), Leadership separate, Peer feedback, Focus groups, Pre/post assessment, 1-1 follow-ups

**Source:** https://www.sessionlab.com/methods/team-self-assessment

### 4. Marshmallow Challenge

18-minute game where teams build tallest free-standing structure using spaghetti, tape, string, and marshmallow.

**Purpose:** Demonstrate power of prototyping over planning. Reveal team problem-solving approaches. Show value of early iteration and testing. Build team collaboration under pressure. Introduce design thinking experientially.

**Group Size:** Per team: 3-6 (optimal 4-5), Total: 2-40 participants
**Time:** 30-120 minutes total (5 min setup + 3 min rules + 18 min building + 5 min measurement + 10-20 min debrief)

**Materials Per Team:**
- 20 spaghetti sticks
- 1 meter masking tape
- 1 meter string
- 1 marshmallow
- 1 paper bag/envelope
- Measuring tape (shared)
- Timer

**Pre-Event Preparation:**
1. Assemble kits in paper bags (for surprise factor)
2. Clear open floor space for building
3. Test measuring equipment
4. Play upbeat music ready

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Setup (5 minutes)** — Divide into teams of 3-6. Assign distinct building spaces. Place unopened kit bags. Seat teams.

2. **Rules Briefing (3 minutes)** — Announce the challenge and key rules:
   - **Tallest:** Structure measured from ground to top of marshmallow
   - **Free-standing:** Cannot be held by person (must stand on its own)
   - **Complete marshmallow:** Entire marshmallow must be on top
   - **Can use materials:** Spaghetti, tape, string only
   - **Can break materials:** Bend, break, tear as needed
   - **Cannot hold:** Supporting structure with hands not allowed at finish
   - **Bag stays out:** Cannot use paper bag in final structure
   - **Time limit:** Exactly 18 minutes (no negotiation)
   - Emphasize: "Questions now? Once I say 'Go,' all questions are done."

3. **Building Phase (18 minutes)** — Say "Go!" Start timer. Place music. Call out time at: 12, 9, 7, 5, 3, 2, 1 minutes and 30-second countdown. Observe team dynamics. Announce team progress. Enforce rules. Call clear **STOP** at 18 minutes exactly.

4. **Measurement & Winner (5 minutes)** — Walk around with measuring tape. Measure each structure. Announce heights and identify winning team. Celebrate with applause.

5. **Reflection & Debrief (15-20 minutes)**:
   - **What Happened? (3-5 min)** — "How did you approach this?" Listen for planning vs. prototyping.
   - **Process Reflection (5-10 min)** — "How did we work as a group?" "Who led at different moments?" "How did we handle failure?"
   - **Key Insight (3-5 min)** — "Kindergarteners typically outperform business professionals. Why? Kindergarteners prototype and test early. Business professionals plan extensively then build once. Prototyping beats planning. Early testing reveals what works."
   - **Transfer (3-5 min)** — "How does this apply to our real work?" "Where do we over-plan instead of testing?" "Where could we prototype faster?"

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Manage time strictly (18 minutes exactly; no extensions)
- No coaching during building
- Observe dynamics; note for debrief
- Create energy with music and enthusiasm
- Make safe to fail
- Be specific in debrief referencing actual behaviors
- Connect to organizational reality

**Variations:** Competitive, Collaborative, Timed iterations (three 6-minute rounds), Materials variation, Stakes variation, Debrief-first approach

**Source:** https://www.sessionlab.com/methods/marshmallow-challenge

### 5. Human Spectrogram

Uses physical line on ground as continuum for exploring diverse perspectives. Participants position themselves and explain their positioning.

**Purpose:** Gather diversity of perspectives physically. Help people see where they align and diverge. Explore nuances vs. binary positions. Build empathy by hearing diverse viewpoints. Create visual representation of group opinion.

**Group Size:** 5-50 optimal, Can scale larger with microphone
**Time:** 20-30 minutes (2 min intro + 3-5 min per question × 3-7 questions)
**Materials:** One long piece of masking tape (3-10 meters), Tape dispenser, Optional: Signs marking ends, Open floor space, Optional: Microphone

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Setup (5 minutes)** — Clear floor space. Lay long masking tape horizontally. Optional: Add signs defining spectrum ends. Gather group around tape. Explain you stand beside tape at appropriate position (not ON tape).

2. **Introduction (2 minutes)** — "This tape is a continuum. One end represents [position A], the other represents [position B]. You'll position yourself based on where you fall on the spectrum. There's no right or wrong position—it's about where you genuinely land."

3. **Demo Question (2-3 minutes)** — Ask fun, low-stakes demo: "I love chocolate" Have people position. Point out different positions. Ask 2-3 people why they positioned where they did. Model brief answer.

4. **Transition (1 minute)** — "Now we move to real questions. You can move if hearing someone changes your mind. This is about understanding, not absolute positions."

5. **Real Questions (5 minutes each)** — Pose substantive question: "How much do you agree that [statement]?" People position. Facilitator walks line asking why. Sample from various spots. Note patterns. Encourage movement if thinking shifts.

6. **Additional Questions (5 minutes each)** — Repeat with 2-6 more questions, progressing from easier to complex. After each question: acknowledge diversity, note patterns.

**Question Examples:**

**Organization/Team:**
- "This organization values innovation more than stability"
- "I feel safe being vulnerable with my team"
- "Remote work is better than in-office for my productivity"
- "Our leadership listens to our input"
- "Change happens too quickly in this organization"

**Product/Service:**
- "This product meets our customers' core needs"
- "We move fast enough to compete"
- "We have clear strategic direction"

**Cultural:**
- "Diversity and inclusion is prioritized in decision-making"
- "People from different backgrounds have equal opportunity here"
- "We are making progress on our climate goals"

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Design strong questions; clarity matters
- Create psychological safety: "All positions are valid"
- Sample broadly when asking "why"
- Allow repositioning: "If hearing someone changes your thinking, move!"
- Watch for power dynamics (leaders' positions can anchor others)
- Notice patterns: Synthesize after each question
- Build on responses: "Why did you say that?" "Who feels differently?"

**Variations:** Live polling (everyone positions simultaneously), Walk and talk, Fishbowl style, Written follow-up, Anonymous input (cards instead of standing), Digital version (online polling or chat, 1-10 scale)

**Source:** https://www.sessionlab.com/methods/human-spectrogram

## WORKSHOP DESIGN FOR HANDS-ON LEARNING

Hands-on workshops teach a skill or build something concrete in a short window. They sit at the intersection of teaching and doing: too much demo and people stay passive, too little structure and they get lost. This section covers workshop design (especially for AI and coding work), pair and mob programming formats, and show & tell closing structures.

### Workshop Design for Build Sessions

#### Structure & Duration

**Standard format**:
- 3-4 hour intensive session
- Small groups (8-20 participants optimal)
- Mix of demo, guided practice, and independent building

**Compressed format** (for hackathons):
- 90-minute deep dive
- Focus on specific use case or tool
- Participants bring own projects to apply learning

#### Key Components

1. **Problem context** (10 min)
   - Real-world problem you're solving
   - Why this tool or approach helps
   - Expected outcomes

2. **Tool walkthrough** (15-20 min)
   - Demo of the specific tool/API
   - Show 2-3 practical examples
   - Demonstrate common pitfalls and how to avoid them

3. **Guided practice** (15-20 min)
   - Participants follow along
   - Build simple first example together
   - Instructor provides step-by-step guidance
   - Shared code repository or starter template

4. **Hands-on building** (30-45 min)
   - Participants build their own projects
   - Apply tool to their use case or problem
   - Peer collaboration encouraged
   - Instructor circulates for support

5. **Sharing & reflection** (10-15 min)
   - Show and tell of what people built
   - Share learning insights
   - Discuss what worked and challenges
   - Distribute resources for continued learning

#### Resource Preparation

**Before the workshop**:
- Create starter template or boilerplate code
- Prepare working examples
- Test all tools and APIs beforehand
- Have backup internet/API solutions
- Create detailed documentation (in-workshop and after)
- Prepare troubleshooting guide for common errors

**During the workshop**:
- Provide printed cheat sheets
- Share code repository link
- Screen-share demos clearly
- Have sample projects for people to fork
- Set expectations for learning pace

### Pair & Mob Programming Formats

#### Pair Programming

**Definition**: Two developers work together at one workstation on a single task, with defined roles:

- **Driver**: Types code, implements logic
- **Navigator**: Reviews code as it's written, spots errors, provides feedback, suggests improvements
- **Rotation**: Partners swap roles regularly (every 20-30 minutes)

**Benefits**:
- Significantly improves code quality through immediate review
- Increases knowledge sharing and mentoring
- Reduces design mistakes before they become expensive
- Builds camaraderie and shared ownership

**Best practices**:
- Set timer for role rotation
- Both partners stay engaged and present
- Create safe space for questions and suggestions
- Pairing works best with one person more experienced than the other
- Pair junior developers with more experienced ones

**With AI assistance**:
- Use AI tools (ChatGPT, GitHub Copilot, Claude) as third "voice" in pair
- AI can generate code suggestions; pair decides whether to accept
- Human pair maintains control of direction and quality
- Pair debugging and validating AI-generated code together
- Use AI for research and documentation lookup

#### Ensemble (Mob) Programming

**Definition**: Entire team works together on a single codebase, with everyone contributing to the same screen/shared workspace.

**Roles**:
- **Driver**: Implements current task, types code
- **Navigators**: Provide direction and strategy
- **Ensemble**: Everyone contributes thinking and ideas

**Format**:
- All team members visible on video call
- Single shared screen (driver broadcasts)
- Typed pseudocode or outline before implementation
- Full group discusses approach before coding

**Benefits**:
- Highest knowledge sharing
- Faster learning and skill development
- Reduced defects (continuous review)
- Strong team cohesion
- Faster onboarding of new team members

**With AI**:
- Use AI to generate test cases while team codes implementation
- AI refactors code suggestions from human ensemble
- Use AI for rapid documentation of ensemble's decisions
- AI assists with edge case and error handling thinking

#### Show & Tell Format

**Purpose**: Participants demonstrate what they built and what they learned.

**Structure**:
- 2-5 minutes per person/team
- Show the working prototype
- Explain key learning or technical choice
- Briefly discuss challenges and solutions
- Audience asks questions

**Best practices**:
- Set clear time limits
- Encourage questions
- Create psychological safety (all attempts valued)
- Record shows (participants and asynchronous viewers)
- Give positive feedback to all presenters
- Document learnings across all presentations

**Source:** Adapted from hackathon and build-event organizer practices.

## MINIMUM VIABLE COMMUNITY (MVC) — 9-QUESTION TOOL

**Purpose:** Rapidly validate community viability and get core stakeholder alignment before investing in full community design. A scaled-down Community Canvas focused on the most critical questions.

**Group Size:** 3-5 core stakeholders
**Time:** 30-45 minutes
**Materials:** One-page worksheet (paper or digital), markers, optional whiteboard for synthesis

**Step-by-Step Facilitation:**

1. **Frame the session (5 min)** — Explain that the goal is to capture community DNA in nine answers, not to design the community fully. Permission to be rough. Encourage gut answers over polished ones.

2. **Work through the nine questions in order (25-30 min)** — Roughly 3 minutes per question. Use silent writing first, then surface answers as a group. Capture each answer in one or two sentences on the worksheet.

   1. **Purpose** — Why does this community exist?
   2. **Audience** — Who is this for?
   3. **Values** — What principles matter most?
   4. **Goals** — What's success in 12 months?
   5. **Shared Experience** — What happens regularly that brings us together?
   6. **Key Rituals** — What traditions or repeated practices have meaning?
   7. **Organization** — How are we organized? Who holds key roles?
   8. **Governance** — How do we make decisions?
   9. **Platforms** — What tools/technology do we use?

3. **Synthesize and gut-check (5-10 min)** — Read all nine answers back. Ask: "Does this feel like a community we'd want to be part of?" Tweak as needed. Identify the one or two answers you're least sure about — those are where deeper planning matters most.

**Facilitator Tips:**
- Don't let any one question consume more than ~5 minutes. The point is alignment, not perfection.
- Watch for the "shared experience" and "key rituals" questions — these are where groups often realize they don't yet have anything concrete to anchor the community in.
- The output is a starting point, not a finished design. Set expectations accordingly.

**When to use MVC:**
- Just starting and need to validate before deeper planning
- Rapid prototyping a new community
- Resource-constrained (limited time, budget, or stakeholder bandwidth)
- Getting initial stakeholder agreement quickly
- Pre-work before running a full Community Canvas workshop

**Expanding to the full Community Canvas (17 themes):** After validating with MVC, layer in brand and communication strategy, member recruitment and onboarding, member transitions and lifecycle, content and knowledge strategy, rules and norms, sustainability and resources, and data and impact measurement. See `community-frameworks.md` for the full Canvas treatment.

**Source:** https://community-canvas.org (Community Canvas team, derived from the full 17-theme framework)
