# Community Design Frameworks

Reference for building communities of practice, learning cohorts, and ongoing groups. Use this file when the user is designing for an ongoing group, not a one-off event.

**Canonical frameworks** (full treatment):
1. **Community Canvas** — 17-theme comprehensive design framework (community-canvas.org)
2. **Community Weaving** — 5-pattern relational framework (community-weaving.org)
3. **Wenger Communities of Practice** — the canonical CoP model (wenger-trayner.com)

**Condensed frameworks** (overview + external pointer):
4. **Tacit Maturity Model** — 4-stage CoP progression assessment (hellotacit.com)
5. **JRC Communities of Practice Playbook** — 8-facet EU implementation guide (op.europa.eu/jrc)

**Complementary frameworks** (brief):
- CMX SPACES Model (cmxhub.com)
- Charles Vogl, *The Art of Community* — 7 principles
- FeverBee Community Lifecycle (feverbee.com)
- Get Together / People & Company (gettogether.world)

> **Note on Minimum Viable Community (MVC)**: The 9-question rapid-start tool from the Community Canvas team is a tactical instrument, not a major framework. It lives in `facilitation-methods.md` under planning tools, alongside IDOARRT and 15% Solutions.

## FRAMEWORK 1: COMMUNITY CANVAS

**Source:** https://community-canvas.org
**Developers:** Fabian Pfortmüller, Nico Luchsinger, Sascha Mombartz
**License:** Creative Commons BY-NC-SA 4.0
**Published:** 2017
**Best For:** Comprehensive community planning, diagnosis, and design workshops

### Framework Structure

The Community Canvas organizes community design into **3 sections** and **17 themes** that progress from foundational identity through member experience to operational sustainability.

### The 3 Sections (with 17 themes)

#### SECTION 1: IDENTITY (5 themes - The Core)

**Why Identity First:** Identity forms the foundation that influences every other decision. It answers "Who are we?" and "What do we believe in?"

**Themes:**

1. **Purpose & Goal**
   - What's the fundamental reason this community exists?
   - What void does it fill?
   - What problem does it solve?
   - Guiding question: "Why do we exist?"

2. **Member Identity**
   - Who is this community for?
   - What characteristics unite potential members?
   - What identity do members adopt in the community?
   - Guiding question: "Who are we as a group?"

3. **Values**
   - What principles and beliefs define the community?
   - What behaviors do we emphasize or discourage?
   - What matters most to us collectively?
   - Guiding question: "What do we believe in?"

4. **Success & Metrics**
   - How do we define and measure success?
   - What are our key performance indicators?
   - What does progress look like?
   - Guiding question: "How will we know if we're succeeding?"

5. **Brand & Communication**
   - How is the community known externally?
   - What's our visual identity and narrative?
   - What language and tone do we use?
   - Guiding question: "What's our community brand?"

#### SECTION 2: EXPERIENCE (6 themes - Member Perspective)

**Why Experience Second:** Experience translates identity into concrete activities and interactions. It answers "What actually happens here?" and "What value do members receive?"

**Themes:**

6. **Member Selection & Recruitment**
   - How do people discover the community?
   - What's the onboarding process?
   - Are there barriers to entry or specific criteria?
   - Guiding question: "How do members join?"

7. **Member Journey & Transitions**
   - What's the path from newcomer to established member?
   - How do members progress through different roles?
   - What marks transitions between levels?
   - Guiding question: "How does membership evolve?"

8. **Shared Experiences**
   - What collective activities bring members together?
   - What creates bonds between members?
   - What are signature community experiences?
   - Guiding question: "What shared experiences create belonging?"

9. **Rituals & Traditions**
   - What regular practices have special meaning?
   - What traditions reinforce community identity?
   - What do we do repeatedly with intention?
   - Guiding question: "What rituals connect us?"

10. **Content & Knowledge Sharing**
    - What information or resources does the community maintain?
    - What stories or case studies do we capture?
    - How is knowledge organized and accessible?
    - Guiding question: "What knowledge does our community steward?"

11. **Roles & Participation**
    - What different ways can members engage?
    - What roles exist from passive observer to core leader?
    - How visible and clear are these options?
    - Guiding question: "How can people participate?"

12. **Rules & Norms**
    - What explicit or implicit guidelines shape behavior?
    - What's encouraged and what's prohibited?
    - How are conflicts resolved?
    - Guiding question: "What are our community norms?"

#### SECTION 3: STRUCTURE (6 themes - Operational Foundation)

**Why Structure Last:** Structure provides the organizational backbone that sustains communities over time. It answers "How do we keep this going?" and "What supports our long-term viability?"

**Themes:**

13. **Organization & Roles**
    - How is the community formally organized?
    - What are key roles and responsibilities?
    - Who reports to whom?
    - Guiding question: "What's our organizational structure?"

14. **Governance & Decision-Making**
    - Who has decision-making authority?
    - What's the decision-making process?
    - How are members included in governance?
    - Guiding question: "How do we make decisions?"

15. **Channels & Platforms**
    - What technology platforms do we use?
    - How do members communicate?
    - What's the digital infrastructure?
    - Guiding question: "What tools enable us?"

16. **Resources & Financing**
    - How is the community funded?
    - What resources are required?
    - What are the cost drivers?
    - Guiding question: "How do we sustain ourselves financially?"

17. **Data & Impact Measurement**
    - What metrics do we track?
    - How do we measure engagement and impact?
    - What data informs decisions?
    - Guiding question: "How do we know our impact?"

### Minimum Viable Community (MVC) — see `facilitation-methods.md`

The MVC is a 9-question rapid-start tool from the Community Canvas team. It compresses the full 17-theme Canvas into the most critical questions for stakeholders, runnable as a 30-45 minute workshop with 3-5 people. The full step-by-step lives in `facilitation-methods.md` under planning tools.

### How to Facilitate a Community Canvas Workshop

**Duration:** 90-180 minutes
**Participants:** 4-8 core stakeholders
**Materials:** Printed or digital canvas, sticky notes, markers, facilitation guide

**Facilitation Steps:**

1. **Introduction (10 min)**
   - Explain the framework
   - Set context: "We're defining what makes our community work"
   - Show how sections relate

2. **Identity Phase (30 min)**
   - Guide group through 5 identity themes
   - Start with Purpose, then build outward
   - Encourage honest conversation about "why"
   - Document decisions on canvas

3. **Experience Phase (40 min)**
   - Walk through member journey
   - Focus on signature experiences and rituals
   - Identify key content and knowledge assets
   - Establish core rules/norms

4. **Structure Phase (40 min)**
   - Address organization and roles
   - Define governance approach
   - Explore financing options
   - Select platforms

5. **Synthesis (20 min)**
   - Review completed canvas
   - Identify priority actions
   - Assign responsibilities for implementation

**Facilitation Tips:**
- Ground the conversation in Identity (why) before tactics
- Use Miro or Mural for distributed teams
- Revisit annually or after major changes
- Adapt language to your audience
- Don't overthink—capture honest essentials

**Outputs:**
- One-page visual summary
- Decision documentation
- Implementation roadmap
- Stakeholder alignment

## FRAMEWORK 2: COMMUNITY WEAVING

**Source:** https://www.community-weaving.org/
**Stewards:** Together Institute (https://www.together-institute.org)
**Creators:** Fabian Pfortmüller, Michel Bachmann, Erin Dixon, Sita Magnuson
**Contributors:** 100+ community weavers
**License:** Creative Commons BY-NC-SA 4.0
**Best For:** Relational mapping, network dynamics, connection-centered design

### Framework Philosophy

Community Weaving reframes community building from project management to relational, organic weaving. It emphasizes that communities are fundamentally about **connection**—how people relate, support, and grow together.

### The 5 Patterns

#### Pattern 1: FIRE (The Possibility That Brings Us Together)

**What it is:** Shared vision, purpose, or possibility that unites and inspires community members.

**Key Elements:**
- Compelling shared aspiration
- Clear articulation of "why we're here"
- Inspiring vision for change
- Sense of possibility and hope

**Questions to ask:**
- What draws people to us?
- What possibility are we holding together?
- Why does this matter?
- What change do we want to create?

**Indicators of Healthy Fire:**
- Purpose is clearly articulated and understood
- Members feel inspired and committed
- Shared vision transcends individual benefit
- Energy and passion are visible

**How to Strengthen Fire:**
- Regularly articulate and reconnect to purpose
- Share stories that illustrate the vision
- Celebrate progress toward goals
- Invite feedback and evolution of purpose

---

#### Pattern 2: WEB (The Relationships That Hold Us Together)

**What it is:** The relational connections, trust, and networks that form the community's foundation.

**Key Elements:**
- Strong, meaningful relationships
- Trust and psychological safety
- Diverse connections (not just like-minded people)
- Mutual care and support

**Questions to ask:**
- Are relationships strong and genuine?
- Do members know and trust each other?
- Is there cross-group connection?
- Do diverse voices feel welcome?

**Network Topologies (adapted from June Holley):**

**Hub & Spoke** (Emerging)
- One central connector (the weaver)
- Weaver connected to multiple groups
- Information flows through hub
- Weaver has external connections
- Strength: Centralization gives shape
- Weakness: Fragility if hub disconnects

**Bridged Network** (Developing)
- Multiple hubs emerging
- Bridges connecting clusters
- Shared responsibility
- More resilient structure
- Strength: Shared leadership
- Weakness: More complex coordination

**Core/Periphery** (Mature)
- Strong core group with deep connections
- Radiating circles of engagement
- Multiple entry points
- Stable, sustainable structure
- Strength: Stability and scalability
- Weakness: Risk of exclusivity

**Network Weaver Roles:**
- **Connectors/Catalysts** - Bring people together, initiate new connections
- **Guardians** - Protect culture, maintain values, watch for burnout
- **Coordinators** - Organize logistics, manage communications
- **Facilitators** - Guide conversations, facilitate learning and growth

**How to Strengthen Web:**
- Intentionally create connection spaces
- Recognize and support connectors
- Create bridge opportunities between clusters
- Celebrate relationships and friendships
- Build in "soft connection" time (not just task-focused)

---

#### Pattern 3: RHYTHM (The Rituals That Connect Us)

**What it is:** Regular practices, gatherings, and cadences that maintain community bonds and visibility.

**Key Elements:**
- Predictable meeting schedule
- Mix of gathering sizes (large, small, intimate)
- Regular communication rhythm
- Meaningful transitions and marking time

**Questions to ask:**
- What regular rituals hold us together?
- How often do we connect?
- What rhythms feel right for our community?
- How do we mark time together?

**Types of Rhythm:**

**Gathering Rhythm:**
- Regular meetings (weekly, monthly, quarterly)
- Larger convenings (conferences, retreats, summits)
- Informal touchpoints (office hours, coffee chats)
- Seasonal events and celebrations

**Communication Rhythm:**
- Email cadence (newsletters, updates)
- Synchronous check-ins (calls, meetings)
- Asynchronous updates (messages, documents)
- Social media engagement patterns

**Activity Rhythm:**
- Seasonal initiatives
- Project cycles
- Learning modules or courses
- Celebration and marking milestones

**Healthy Rhythm Indicators:**
- Members know when to expect gatherings
- Attendance is consistent
- Rituals feel meaningful and valued
- Rhythm sustains engagement without overwhelming

**How to Strengthen Rhythm:**
- Establish consistent, predictable cadences
- Build variety (large/small, formal/informal, sync/async)
- Include transitions and marking of time
- Adapt rhythm as community evolves
- Honor different participation styles (some prefer large, some small)

---

#### Pattern 4: CIRCLES (The Roles We Can Play)

**What it is:** Recognition that community members can participate in diverse ways, with different roles, responsibilities, and levels of involvement.

**Key Elements:**
- Multiple pathways for participation
- Clear role options visible to members
- Ability to deepen involvement over time
- Recognition of different contribution types

**Questions to ask:**
- What roles exist in our community?
- How do people move between roles?
- What pathways exist for deeper engagement?
- How do we recognize different contributions?

**Circle Structure (Concentric Model):**

**Core Circle** (5-8 people)
- Founders, stewards, primary caretakers
- High commitment and involvement
- Leadership and decision-making roles
- Regular engagement (weekly or more)

**Active Circle** (20-30% of community)
- Regular participants and volunteers
- Committee members and project leads
- Consistent engagement (bi-weekly/monthly)
- Visible contributions

**Peripheral Circle** (60-70% of community)
- Occasional participants
- Observers and learners
- Event attendees
- Low or seasonal engagement

**Role Categories:**

1. **Stewardship Roles**
   - Vision holders, long-term caretakers
   - Community historians, culture guardians
   - Strategic advisors

2. **Operational Roles**
   - Organizers, event planners, coordinators
   - Communicators, newsletter writers
   - Technology and platform managers

3. **Content Roles**
   - Teachers, subject matter experts
   - Facilitators, discussion leaders
   - Knowledge organizers, researchers

4. **Support & Connection Roles**
   - Welcomers, onboarders, greeters
   - Mentors and coaches
   - Connectors, bridgers between groups
   - Listeners and emotional support

5. **Participatory Roles**
   - Regular attendees and participants
   - Contributors and sharers
   - Learners and observers
   - Resource providers

**How to Strengthen Circles:**
- Make roles explicit and visible
- Create clear pathways for growth
- Recognize and honor all contributions
- Rotate leadership to develop capacity
- Support transitions as people evolve
- Create "edge" roles for those wanting challenge

---

#### Pattern 5: SPIRAL (Our Individual and Collective Journeys)

**What it is:** Recognition that both communities and individual members are constantly evolving, learning, and transforming.

**Key Elements:**
- Continuous learning at individual and community level
- Repeated cycles at deeper levels (not linear)
- Transformation and growth over time
- Graceful transitions and evolution

**Questions to ask:**
- How is our community evolving?
- How are individuals growing through participation?
- What transformation is happening?
- How do we honor different paces of change?

**Spiral Dynamics:**

**Repeated Cycles (Not Linear Progress)**
- Same themes return at deeper levels
- Learning spirals upward
- Communities revisit purpose, structures, rhythms
- Growth is iterative, not linear

**Individual Spirals**
- Member learning and skill development
- Deepening expertise and contribution
- Personal transformation through community
- Life trajectory changes (roles evolve)

**Community Spirals**
- Evolution of purpose (refined, expanded, focused)
- Maturation of structure (informal → formal)
- Scaling dynamics (growing while maintaining connection)
- Legacy and succession planning

**Transformation Spirals**
- Adaptation to external changes
- Responding to member feedback
- Evolving culture and norms
- Emergence of new sub-communities or phases

**Indicators of Healthy Spiral:**
- Continuous learning visible in community
- Members cite personal growth and transformation
- Community improves and adapts over time
- New generations of leaders emerge
- Purpose deepens and evolves intentionally

**How to Strengthen Spiral:**
- Create space for reflection and learning
- Celebrate member growth and transformation stories
- Enable graceful transitions between roles
- Adapt community structure as it evolves
- Build mentorship and succession planning
- Mark and honor major transitions

---

### How to Use Community Weaving

#### 2-3 Hour Assessment Process

**Step 1: Fire Assessment (15 min)**
- Is purpose clear and compelling?
- Do all members understand the "why"?
- Is there shared commitment?
- Is purpose evolving appropriately?

**Step 2: Web Assessment (30 min)**
- Map current relationships and connections
- Identify connectors and bridges
- Assess trust levels
- Note any isolated members or groups
- What's the current topology?

**Step 3: Rhythm Assessment (20 min)**
- What regular rituals exist?
- Is the cadence adequate and sustainable?
- Do members know what to expect?
- Are there gaps in connection?

**Step 4: Circles Assessment (20 min)**
- What roles are visible?
- How clear are participation pathways?
- Who's in each circle?
- Are roles valued equally?

**Step 5: Spiral Assessment (15 min)**
- How is the community evolving?
- What learning is happening?
- Are new leaders emerging?
- How are transitions managed?

**Step 6: Prioritization & Planning (15 min)**
- Which patterns need most attention?
- What quick wins exist?
- What requires deeper work?
- What's the first action?

#### Community Weaving Canvas Workshop

**Time:** 2-3 hours
**Participants:** 5-15 community members (mix of roles)
**Materials:** Miro template, sticky notes, facilitator guide

**Flow:**
1. Introduce Five Patterns (15 min)
2. Assess each pattern (50 min total, ~10 min per)
3. Identify top 2-3 priorities (20 min)
4. Design experiments/interventions (25 min)
5. Commit to actions and next steps (10 min)

## FRAMEWORK 3: WENGER COMMUNITIES OF PRACTICE

**Source:** https://www.wenger-trayner.com/
**Theorists:** Etienne Wenger, Jean Lave
**Key Works:**
- "Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity" (1998)
- "Cultivating Communities of Practice: A Guide to Managing Knowledge" (2002)
**Best For:** Professional learning communities, knowledge management, organizational development

### Framework Foundation

Wenger's framework is grounded in social learning theory and describes how communities naturally emerge around domains of practice. CoPs are powerful engines of learning where people develop expertise together.

### The Three Essential Pillars

All three pillars must be present for a true CoP to exist:

#### Pillar 1: DOMAIN

**Definition:** A shared domain of interest or expertise that gives the community its identity.

**Characteristics:**
- Members have a common domain or topic
- Membership implies commitment to the domain
- Shared competence distinguishes members
- Domain creates common ground and identity

**Examples of Domains:**
- Software architecture in large tech companies
- Clinical diagnosis and treatment in hospitals
- Classroom management in schools
- Change management in large organizations
- Product innovation in design firms

**Domain Development Trajectory:**
- **Potential:** Shared interest or problem emerges
- **Evolving:** Shared competence developing
- **Maturing:** Deep expertise and recognition
- **Stewarding:** Knowledge codified and passed on

**Assessment Questions:**
- What's the core domain?
- Is it compelling and valuable?
- Do all members understand the scope?
- Is there genuine shared expertise?
- How is the domain evolving?

---

#### Pillar 2: COMMUNITY

**Definition:** Built through joint activities, mutual help, and relationships that enable learning.

**Characteristics:**
- Members engage in joint activities
- Mutual support and help
- Shared information and knowledge
- Strong relationships built over time
- Trust and reciprocity
- Sense of belonging

**Three Levels of Participation:**

**Core Group** (10-15% of members)
- Frequent interaction
- Drive innovation and direction
- Maintain continuity
- Mentor to others
- Full commitment

**Active Members** (20-30%)
- Regular participation
- Attend events, contribute knowledge
- Take on projects or initiatives
- Visible presence
- Moderate commitment

**Peripheral Members** (55-70%)
- Occasional participation
- Often in learning mode
- May not attend regularly
- Still benefit from membership
- Low-moderate commitment

**Assessment Questions:**
- Are relationships strong?
- Is there trust?
- Are different levels of participation honored?
- Do members feel belonging?
- Is there mutual support?
- Are newcomers welcomed?

**How to Build Community:**
- Create regular touchpoints
- Design collaborative work
- Encourage informal interaction
- Enable mentoring relationships
- Build psychological safety
- Celebrate achievements together

---

#### Pillar 3: PRACTICE

**Definition:** Shared repertoire of resources, approaches, and knowledge developed together.

**Elements of Shared Practice:**
- Experiences and stories (lessons learned, war stories)
- Tools and techniques (templates, processes, software)
- Language and concepts (terminology, frameworks, acronyms)
- Norms and values (how we work, what we care about)
- Approaches to solving problems
- Best practices and standards
- Documentation and knowledge bases

**Examples:**
- A software architecture CoP shares design patterns, code examples, technology evaluations
- A teaching CoP shares lesson plans, assessment tools, classroom management strategies
- A clinical CoP shares diagnostic approaches, treatment protocols, case studies

**Assessment Questions:**
- What tools does our practice use?
- What knowledge are we building?
- What stories are we capturing?
- How is knowledge documented?
- What makes our practice distinctive?
- How is practice evolving?

**How to Develop Practice:**
- Explicitly document tools and approaches
- Capture and share stories
- Create shared vocabulary
- Develop templates and frameworks
- Hold case study discussions
- Create best practice libraries

---

### The Three Dimensions of Practice

Beyond the pillars, Wenger identifies three dimensions that strengthen community cohesion:

#### Dimension 1: MUTUAL ENGAGEMENT

**Meaning:** Members are actively engaged with each other, not just with the topic.

**What it looks like:**
- Regular interaction and communication
- Willingness to help and support
- Engagement is bidirectional
- Connections form naturally through work
- People seek each other out

**How to develop:**
- Create regular touchpoints (meetings, calls)
- Design collaborative projects
- Encourage informal interaction
- Enable peer-to-peer learning
- Build one-on-one mentoring

---

#### Dimension 2: JOINT ENTERPRISE

**Meaning:** Members work toward shared goals or respond to shared challenges.

**What it looks like:**
- Shared purpose and direction
- Collective sense of responsibility
- Members feel ownership
- Community defines what it cares about
- Healthy debate within shared commitment

**How to develop:**
- Clarify shared problems or goals
- Create space for negotiation
- Involve members in direction-setting
- Celebrate collective achievements
- Allow healthy debate

---

#### Dimension 3: SHARED REPERTOIRE

**Meaning:** Common resources, tools, knowledge, and language accumulated over time.

**What it looks like:**
- Observable set of artifacts and tools
- Accumulated wisdom and experience
- Common language and concepts
- Both documented and undocumented knowledge
- Evolving based on practice

**How to develop:**
- Capture and share stories
- Create documentation
- Develop shared tools
- Establish terminology
- Celebrate knowledge contributions

---

### The Five Lifecycle Stages

Communities of Practice naturally evolve through distinct stages. Understanding the stage helps guide appropriate interventions.

#### Stage 1: POTENTIAL (Seed)

**What's happening:**
- Informal group with shared interest
- Topic or problem attracts attention
- People begin to notice common ground
- Not yet formally existing

**Duration:** Pre-launch to early months

**Characteristics:**
- Loose, informal connections
- Emerging awareness of shared interest
- Individual expertise not yet pooled
- High uncertainty about viability

**Key Activities:**
- Initial conversations and connections
- Identifying common interest
- Reaching critical mass (5-10 people)
- Building initial enthusiasm

**What's Needed:**
- A champion or initiator
- Safe space to explore
- Initial communication channel
- Early wins to build momentum

**Progression Criteria:**
- Real shared interest confirmed
- Key people committed
- Initial meeting scheduled
- Basic communication channel established

---

#### Stage 2: COALESCING (Building)

**What's happening:**
- Energy and enthusiasm peak
- Trust begins to form
- Common purpose becoming clear
- Early learning and contribution
- Community gaining definition

**Duration:** First 6-18 months

**Characteristics:**
- Regular meetings and interactions
- Trust-building through vulnerability
- Clarity around purpose emerging
- Early knowledge sharing
- Excitement about possibilities

**Key Activities:**
- Establish regular meeting rhythm
- Shared projects and initiatives
- Building trust through shared work
- Identifying knowledge gaps
- Clarifying mission and values

**What's Needed:**
- Regular convening
- Clear communication
- Leadership visibility
- Early documentation
- Celebration of progress

**Progression Criteria:**
- Trust visibly present
- Clear purpose articulated
- Regular participation established
- Knowledge sharing begun
- Leaders identified

---

#### Stage 3: MATURING (Defining)

**What's happening:**
- Identity and boundaries clarified
- Role clarity and structure
- Process maturation
- Knowledge gaps identified
- Potential plateauing or renewed energy

**Duration:** 1-3+ years

**Characteristics:**
- Formalization of processes
- Expanded membership
- More sophisticated knowledge management
- Clear roles and responsibilities
- Sustainable rhythm

**Key Activities:**
- Formalize processes and roles
- Expand membership intentionally
- Create knowledge management systems
- Address scaling challenges
- Deepen expertise

**What's Needed:**
- Clear governance and roles
- Knowledge management systems
- Leadership pipeline
- Formalized communication
- Sustainability planning

**Progression Criteria:**
- Clear governance in place
- Sustainable processes established
- Knowledge being systematically shared
- New leaders emerging
- Demonstrable value visible

---

#### Stage 4: STEWARDSHIP (Sustaining)

**What's happening:**
- Stability and sustainability achieved
- New energy needed (risk: stagnation)
- Balance between continuity and innovation
- Bringing in new people and ideas
- Supporting experienced members

**Duration:** 3+ years, often ongoing

**Characteristics:**
- Sustainable structures and processes
- Mentoring of emerging leaders
- Knowledge codification
- Regular evaluation
- Strategic evolution

**Key Activities:**
- Mentor emerging leaders
- Document institutional knowledge
- Bring in fresh perspectives
- Celebrate milestones
- Succession planning

**What's Needed:**
- Intentional renewal strategies
- Leadership succession planning
- New challenge identification
- Community historian role
- Regular assessment

**Progression Criteria:**
- New leaders developed
- Knowledge captured
- Fresh energy injected
- Community evolved intentionally
- Long-term viability assured

---

#### Stage 5: TRANSFORMATION (Evolution/Conclusion)

**What's happening:**
- Community evolves to new form or concludes
- Knowledge transfer happening
- Legacy being documented
- Members disperse or redirect

**Duration:** Variable

**Possible Outcomes:**
- Shift to new domain or focus
- Merge with related communities
- Formalize into different structure
- Conclude with gratitude
- Split into sub-communities

**Characteristics:**
- Reflection on journey
- Knowledge documentation
- Celebration of contributions
- Planning next phases

**Key Activities:**
- Document legacy and learnings
- Celebrate community journey
- Transfer knowledge
- Plan next phase
- Honor contributions

**What's Needed:**
- Space for reflection
- Documentation process
- Gratitude and celebration
- Thoughtful transition planning

### Legitimate Peripheral Participation (LPP)

**Framework:** How newcomers become old-timers through gradually increasing participation.

**The Journey:**
```
Newcomer → Increasing Participation → Full Member → Old-Timer (Steward)
```

**Key Characteristics:**

1. **Legitimacy** - Newcomers are welcomed, not excluded; participation is valued
2. **Peripherality** - Start with simple, low-risk, productive tasks; gradual complexity increase
3. **Participation** - Learning happens through doing, not classroom instruction

**How to Enable LPP:**
- Create entry points with simple initial tasks
- Pair newcomers with mentors
- Share how you learned
- Increase responsibility gradually
- Provide multiple participation pathways
- Value and celebrate peripheral participation
- Make tacit knowledge explicit

**Anti-patterns to Avoid:**
- High barriers to entry → elitism
- Rapid responsibility → overwhelm
- No mentoring → isolation
- Invisible learning process → magic, not accessibility
- Core-only value → marginalization of periphery

### Seven Principles for Cultivating Communities of Practice

Research suggests these seven principles support thriving CoPs:

#### 1. DESIGN FOR EVOLUTION

Communities naturally evolve. Don't try to control them rigidly.

**Actions:**
- Allow the community to surprise you
- Revisit purpose periodically
- Be responsive to member feedback
- Expect and embrace evolution
- Build flexibility into structures

#### 2. WELCOME DIFFERENT LEVELS OF PARTICIPATION

Not everyone participates the same way. Honor all levels.

**Actions:**
- Create multiple participation pathways
- Recognize different contribution types
- Don't force people to deepen involvement
- Value observation and learning
- Provide growth opportunities

#### 3. DEVELOP PUBLIC AND PRIVATE SPACES

Communities need both large gatherings and intimate connections.

**Actions:**
- Create large convenings (conferences, summits)
- Enable small group connections
- Encourage one-on-one mentoring
- Support informal networking
- Mix sync and async

#### 4. FOCUS ON VALUE

Communities thrive when members experience concrete value.

**Actions:**
- Identify and articulate value
- Ensure members receive benefits
- Share success stories
- Measure impact
- Evolve offerings based on needs

#### 5. COMBINE FAMILIARITY WITH EXCITEMENT

Balance routine with novelty.

**Actions:**
- Establish regular rhythms
- Introduce new topics/speakers
- Rotate leadership roles
- Try new formats
- Honor traditions while innovating

#### 6. CREATE OPPORTUNITIES FOR DIFFERENT PARTICIPATION STYLES

Not everyone learns the same way.

**Actions:**
- Offer diverse formats (workshops, discussion, panels, online)
- Create async options
- Recognize different contribution types
- Support introverts and extroverts
- Accommodate different schedules

#### 7. MAKE COMMUNITY RHYTHMS VISIBLE

Help members feel the heartbeat of the community.

**Actions:**
- Communicate regular schedules
- Highlight member stories
- Share achievements
- Mark milestones
- Create visible feedback loops

## FRAMEWORK 4: TACIT COMMUNITY OF PRACTICE MATURITY MODEL (Condensed)

**Source:** https://hellotacit.com/community-of-practice-maturity-model/
**Developer:** Tacit (CoP consulting firm)
**Best For:** Assessing where a CoP currently stands and what needs to develop next.

### What It Is

A practical 4-stage progression model and assessment tool for communities of practice. Use it to diagnose maturity, identify capability gaps, and plan a development roadmap. Most useful when you already have a CoP running and want a structured way to ask "what does it need next?"

### The Four Maturity Stages

```
Potential → Forming → Maturing → Self-sustaining
  (Seed)    (Build)   (Develop)    (Sustain)
```

**Stage 1: Potential (Seed)** — Initial awareness of a shared domain. A spark of possibility. Minimal formal structure, unclear governance, ad-hoc communication. Highly dependent on one or two champions. *What's needed next:* domain clarity, an initial sponsor, a first communication channel, a first gathering scheduled, defined success criteria.

**Stage 2: Forming (Build)** — Regular interactions beginning. Core group of 5-8 established. Communication processes emerging. 30-50% of members regularly engaged. Knowledge documentation beginning, informally. *What's needed next:* regular convening rhythm, clearer roles, basic governance, knowledge management practices, leadership recruitment beyond the core few.

**Stage 3: Maturing (Develop)** — Established rhythm. Documented practices. Shared governance. Knowledge artifacts visible to the organization. 50-70% engagement. Value to the organization demonstrable. *What's needed next:* leadership succession beyond founders, structured onboarding for new members, measurement infrastructure, links to organizational decision-making.

**Stage 4: Self-sustaining (Sustain)** — Community runs without dependence on founders. Distributed leadership. Knowledge flows visibly into work. Members onboard members. Measurement informs continuous improvement. *Risk at this stage:* complacency, drift from purpose, becoming an echo chamber. Periodic re-engagement with original "why" is the most common preventive practice.

### Key Assessment Dimensions

Across all four stages, Tacit assesses seven dimensions:

1. **Governance** — From informal to distributed and documented
2. **Leadership** — From single champion to distributed succession
3. **Engagement** — From sporadic to systematic
4. **Knowledge capture** — From none to flowing into organizational work
5. **Communication** — From ad-hoc to multi-channel and rhythmic
6. **Resources** — From minimal to formally allocated
7. **Sustainability** — From person-dependent to community-owned

### How to Run a Maturity Assessment

A 90-120 minute workshop with 4-8 stakeholders works well:
- Brief the model (15 min)
- Rate yourselves 1-4 on each of the 7 dimensions, silently first (20 min)
- Surface scores and discuss disagreements (30-45 min)
- Identify the 2-3 dimensions you most want to advance (15 min)
- Translate those into a 90-day action set (15 min)

### When to Reach for Tacit vs. Other CoP Frameworks

- **Wenger** for foundational understanding of how CoPs work and what cultivation looks like.
- **Tacit** for assessment, progression diagnosis, and roadmap planning.
- **JRC Playbook** for comprehensive enterprise-scale implementation across 8 facets.

- **Tools and templates:** https://hellotacit.com/tools/

## FRAMEWORK 5: JRC COMMUNITIES OF PRACTICE PLAYBOOK (Condensed)

**Source:** https://op.europa.eu/webpub/jrc/communities-of-practice-playbook/en/
**Developer:** European Commission, Joint Research Center (JRC)
**Published:** 2021
**Research Base:** 25+ community manager interviews and surveys across the European Commission
**Best For:** Large organizations, multi-stakeholder CoPs, systematic implementation with strong governance and measurement.

### What It Is

The JRC Playbook is a comprehensive, evidence-based methodology for launching, developing, and sustaining communities of practice inside large organizations. It's particularly strong on governance, facilitation, and measurement.

If your context is a small or informal community, Wenger or Community Canvas will serve better. If you need an enterprise-grade implementation playbook with role definitions, decision rights, and KPI tracking, JRC is the most operational of the CoP frameworks.

### The Eight Success Facets

| Facet | Key Question | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Vision** | Why does this community exist? | Purpose statement, SMART goals, stakeholder map |
| 2. **Governance** | How do we make decisions? | Decision model, roles, accountability, escalation paths |
| 3. **Leadership** | Who leads and how? | Sponsor, community manager, core team, distributed leadership |
| 4. **Convening** | When and how often do we meet? | Event cadence, meeting formats, virtual/in-person mix |
| 5. **Collaboration & Cooperation** | How do we work together? | Shared work practices, co-creation rituals, knowledge artifacts |
| 6. **Community Management** | What's the day-to-day? | Onboarding, moderation, member care, conflict handling |
| 7. **User Experience** | What's it like to be a member? | Journey design, engagement rhythms, recognition |
| 8. **Measurement** | What's the impact? | KPIs, qualitative signals, value-to-organization reporting |

### How to Use It

The Playbook is structured so you can dip in by facet rather than reading linearly. Two common entry points:

- **Launching a new CoP**: Work through Vision → Governance → Leadership → Convening in sequence, then layer in the others.
- **Diagnosing a stuck CoP**: Rate yourselves 1-5 on each of the 8 facets. The lowest score usually reveals the bottleneck.

### When to Reach for JRC vs. Other CoP Frameworks

- **Wenger** for the theoretical foundation and any context where learning-through-practice is the heart of the community.
- **Tacit Maturity Model** for assessment and progression planning.
- **JRC** when you need a comprehensive operational playbook for an enterprise-scale CoP with formal governance and measurement obligations.

### Available Resources

- **Full Playbook (free):** https://op.europa.eu/webpub/jrc/communities-of-practice-playbook/en/
- **Print version and templates:** Available via the JRC publication portal
- Implementation phases, role descriptions, and KPI examples are documented in the source.

### FRAMEWORK 7: CMX SPACES MODEL

**Source:** https://www.cmxhub.com
**Developer:** David Spinks, CMX (Community Marketing Exchange)
**Focus:** Business value and alignment

**The Six Pillars:**
1. **Support** - Members help solve problems (reduce support costs)
2. **Product** - Members provide feedback and ideas
3. **Acquisition** - Community attracts and converts new customers
4. **Content** - Members create valuable user-generated content
5. **Engagement** - Community increases retention and loyalty
6. **Success** - Community enables customer success

**Use When:** Need to justify community investment, align with business goals

---

### FRAMEWORK 8: CHARLES VOGL - ART OF COMMUNITY

**Source:** https://www.charlesvogl.com
**Book:** "The Art of Community: Seven Principles for Belonging"
**Focus:** Creating deep belonging

**The Seven Principles:**
1. **Boundary** - Clear line between members and non-members
2. **Initiation** - Rituals that mark entry into community
3. **Rituals** - Repeated practices with meaning
4. **Temple** - Physical or digital space for community
5. **Stories** - Narratives that teach values
6. **Symbols** - Visual markers of community identity
7. **Inner Rings** - Pathways for deepening participation

**Use When:** Designing for meaningful, deep belonging

---

### FRAMEWORK 9: FEVERBEE COMMUNITY LIFECYCLE

**Source:** https://www.feverbee.com
**Developer:** Richard Millington
**Focus:** Understanding growth patterns

**The Four/Five Stages:**
1. **Inception** (0-3 months) - Reaching critical mass
2. **Establishment** (3-9 months) - Building culture
3. **Maturity** - Plateauing engagement
4. **[Optional] Saturation** - Addressing limits
5. **Mitosis** - Community division into sub-communities

**Use When:** Understanding where in growth cycle, planning for transitions

---

### FRAMEWORK 10: GET TOGETHER / PEOPLE & COMPANY

**Source:** https://gettogether.world
**Developers:** Bailey Richardson, Kevin Huynh, Kai Elmer Sotto
**Focus:** Member-centered co-creation

**The Three Elements:**
1. **Shared Activities** - Purposeful, participatory, repeatable
2. **Community Rituals** - Repeated practices that strengthen bonds
3. **Community Purpose** - Clear articulation of why community exists

**Core Principle:** "Build your community WITH people, not FOR them"

**Use When:** Emphasizing member participation and co-creation
